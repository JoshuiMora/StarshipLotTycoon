# Starship Lot Tycoon
## Game Design Document

---

# High Concept

Starship Lot Tycoon is a cozy business management simulation where players restore and grow a struggling starship dealership into one of the galaxy's most respected retailers.

The game is not about saving the galaxy.

The player is simply a business owner living within it.

Galactic events, politics, wars, scandals, and economic shifts occur independently, creating opportunities and challenges for the player's business.

---

# Core Fantasy

"I built this business."

Players should remember:

- their first ship
- their first customer
- their first profitable flip
- their first employee
- their first campus renovation

The dealership itself becomes a record of the player's journey.

---

# Core Gameplay Loop

1. Browse Marketplace
2. Purchase Ship
3. Wait for Delivery
4. Inspect Ship
5. Decide Restoration Strategy
6. Order Parts
7. Restore Ship
8. Move Ship to Sales Lot
9. Negotiate Sale
10. Collect Profit
11. Reinvest Into Business

Repeat.

---

Cinnematic Focus System

-The game is primarily played from a top down view/management perspective.  Interacting with important people, buildings, and events smoothly transitions the camera into an immersive close-up view, allowing players to experiennce key business moments without leavingn the manangement simulation

---

# Design Philosophy

NON-NEGOTIABLE PRINCIPLES
-The player always approves ship purchases.
-Every ship should tell a story.
-The dealership should visibly evolve over time.
-The galaxy exists independently of the player.
-The first hour should be calm, personal, and memorable.
-New systems must deepend decisions, not replace them.

Automation should remove labor.

Automation should NEVER remove important decisions.

The player always decides:

- what ships to buy
- what repairs to perform
- what upgrades to install
- when to sell
- how to price inventory
- when to expand
- how much financial risk to take

Employees execute the player's decisions.

---

# Starting Experience

The player purchases a run-down dealership consisting of:

- One deteriorating hangar
- Tiny converted storage-room office
- Parts storage
- Small sales lot

The dealership has:

- One repair bay
- Limited storage
- Capacity for one active restoration (later expanding to three)

The player performs all work themselves.

---

# Tutorial

"The First Deal"

The tutorial teaches gameplay naturally through one complete transaction.

Player learns:

- Order replacement parts
- Browse marketplace
- Purchase first ship
- Receive parts delivery
- Receive ship delivery
- Inspect ship
- Replace components
- Start ship
- Prepare sales lot
- Complete first customer negotiation

After the first successful sale the player is turned loose.

---

# Business Progression

Early Game

- One hangar
- One employee (the player)
- One project at a time
- Few customers
- Limited suppliers

Mid Game

- Larger hangar
- Additional storage
- First employees
- Multiple active restorations
- Better purchasing sources

Late Game

- Professional dealership
- Showroom
- Paint shop
- Customization bays
- Procurement office
- Finance office
- Regional reputation

End Game

- Corporate headquarters
- Large inventory
- Fleet sales
- Premium customers
- Industry influence

---

# Employees

Employees reduce workload.

They do not replace gameplay.

Examples:

Mechanic

Repairs ships.

Detailer

Cleans ships.

Painter

Applies paint.

Salesperson

Conducts customer negotiations.

Parts Manager

Orders commonly used parts.

Procurement Office

Unlocks new purchasing sources.

The player still chooses which ships to purchase.

---

# Economy

The game contains a simulated economy.

Players do not see raw market numbers.

Instead they receive information through:

- Galactic news
- Manufacturer announcements
- Economic reports
- Customer demand

Markets naturally fluctuate over time.

Examples:

- Pirate activity
- Mining boom
- Manufacturer scandal
- Border conflict
- Wartime economy

These events influence:

- Ship values
- Parts prices
- Customer demand
- Delivery times

---

# Manufacturers

The game uses an entirely original universe.

No Star Citizen intellectual property will be used.

Manufacturers each possess unique identities.

Examples include:

- Luxury
- Industrial
- Military
- Exploration
- Cargo
- Budget

Brand identity influences:

- Reliability
- Customer demand
- Repair costs
- Reputation

---

# Financial System

Monthly expenses include:

- Payroll
- Utilities
- Loan payments
- Taxes
- Insurance
- Advertising

Players choose how much financial risk to assume.

Conservative players maintain cash reserves.

Aggressive players maximize inventory and debt.

Neither strategy is inherently correct.

---

# Loans

Players begin with a business loan.

Loans may be:

- Paid down early
- Refinanced
- Expanded

Strong financial performance unlocks better financing terms.

Poor performance increases borrowing costs.

---

# Reputation

Reputation represents trust.

Higher reputation results in:

- More customer traffic
- Better financing
- Better purchasing opportunities
- Repeat customers
- Fleet contracts

Reputation grows through honest business practices and successful sales.

---

# Campus

The dealership itself evolves.

Rather than placing dozens of buildings, players renovate and expand an existing property.

Campus upgrades visually reflect business success.

---

# Customer System

Customers possess:

- Budget
- Preferred manufacturers
- Preferred ship types
- Desired features
- Negotiation style

Sales occur through an interactive negotiation system.

Strong salespeople can eventually automate this process.

---

# Core Design Goals

The player should always feel like:

"I made the right business decision."

Rather than:

"My automation made money."

---

# Emotional Journey

Hour 1

"I hope this ship starts."

Hour 5

"I think I can afford another mechanic."

Hour 15

"This renovation is going to change everything."

Hour 30

"I remember when this was just one broken hangar."

Hour 60

"I built one of the best dealerships in the galaxy."

---

# Guiding Principle

Every major mechanic should reinforce one central fantasy:

"I started with nothing, made smart decisions, and built a successful business."
